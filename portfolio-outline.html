Data Science & AI Portfolio Outline for Peyton Ramsey

Overview

This report outlines a concrete structure and content plan for a personal portfolio website showcasing data science, AI engineering, and full‑stack projects for Peyton Ramsey. The goal is to highlight production‑grade ML systems, strong MLOps practices, and domain knowledge across finance, insurance, and fitness in a way that complements, not duplicates, the resume.[^1][^2]

Target Roles and Positioning

Peyton is pursuing roles at the intersection of data science, ML/AI engineering, and analytics engineering, with experience deploying ML systems to production and teaching others how to use AI tools. The portfolio should position Peyton as someone who can own the full lifecycle: framing business problems, building models, deploying them, and making them reliable in production.[^2][^1]

Suggested One‑Line Tagline

  • “Data science and AI engineer focused on production‑ready ML systems, MLOps, and decision‑driven analytics.”

High‑Level Site Structure

Recommended top‑level navigation:

  • Home
  • Projects
  • Experience
  • About
  • Writing (optional but recommended)
  • Contact / Resume

This can be implemented as a simple static site (no need for a heavy framework) and later extended as projects and writing grow.

Hosting and Tech Stack (Lightweight Options)

The portfolio does not need a complex stack; reliability and ease of iteration matter more than technology flashiness.

  • Option A – Static site with GitHub Pages (recommended to start)
    Use a simple static site (plain HTML/CSS or a light generator like Jekyll/Hugo) hosted on GitHub Pages for fast, free deployment and easy version control.
  • Option B – Next.js on Vercel or React SPA
    Consider this if there is a desire to treat the portfolio itself as a React project; good for experimenting with front‑end skills but more setup.

Starting with Option A lets Peyton ship quickly and focus on content; the stack can be upgraded later without changing the content model.

Projects Section – Overall Strategy

The Projects section is the core of the portfolio and should be carefully structured.[^2][^1]

How Many Projects and Depth Per Project

  • 4–6 flagship projects with full detail pages (deep dives).
    Each deep dive can be roughly 700–1200 words plus visuals.
  • Additional 4–10 smaller projects listed in a “More Projects” gallery with short summaries (2–3 sentences each) and tags.

Deep dives should focus on projects that show:

  • End‑to‑end ownership (data to deployment).
  • Clear business impact and evaluation metrics.
  • MLOps / production readiness.
  • Real‑world domain problems (finance, energy, insurance, fitness).[^2][^1]

Suggested Flagship Projects for Deep Dives

Based on existing materials, recommended flagship deep‑dive pages:

  1. Financial News RAG Analyst.
  2. MLOps Loan Default Prediction System with Drift Monitoring.
  3. GymLog – Full‑Stack Fitness Tracking Application.
  4. Airbnb Market Prediction – San Francisco.
  5. Energy Market Data Analyst AI Agent (as a “Work in Progress” page for now).

Standard Project Page Template

Use a consistent structure for all deep‑dive project pages:

  1. Title & Meta
    • Project title.
    • One‑line subtitle (problem in plain language).
    • Role (e.g., “Sole developer” or “Lead developer”).
    • Timeframe (e.g., “December 2025”).[^1]
  2. TL;DR (3–4 sentences)
    • What problem was solved.
    • What was built.
    • Key results (numbers where possible).
    • Where it runs (e.g., “Deployed on Hugging Face Spaces with Docker”).[^1]
  3. Problem & Context (1–2 short paragraphs)
    • Business or user problem.
    • Why it mattered (e.g., research time reduction, compliance visibility, training efficiency).[^2][^1]
  4. Data & Features (bullets)
    • Data sources, size, and key features.
    • Any data challenges (missing data, imbalance, noisy labels).
    • Notable feature engineering steps.
  5. Approach & Architecture
    • High‑level design: pipelines, models, services, and deployment.
    • Simple architecture diagram (image) where helpful.
  6. Modeling / Algorithms
    • Models used and why chosen (Random Forest vs. Logistic Regression, Gradient Boosting, embeddings models, etc.).[^3][^2][^1]
    • Training and validation strategy (cross‑validation, A/B tests, drift monitoring).
    • Key hyperparameters or architecture decisions only if they add insight.
  7. Results & Impact
    • Metrics (accuracy, ROC‑AUC, latency, uptake).
    • Before/after comparison (e.g., “30+ minutes of manual research reduced to <5 seconds”).[^1]
  8. What Was Learned / Challenges
    • 3–5 bullet points on lessons and trade‑offs.
    • Mention any missteps and how they were corrected.
  9. Links & Artifacts
    • GitHub repository.
    • Live demo link.
    • Slide deck or PDF if available (e.g., airbnb‑project‑resume.pdf).[^3]

Example Project Layouts (Personalized)

Financial News RAG Analyst – Deep‑Dive Page

This project is a strong anchor for AI engineering and production RAG systems.[^1]

Suggested content:

  • TL;DR
    Built a production RAG tool that lets users query 500+ financial news articles in natural language, cutting research time from 30+ minutes to under 5 seconds and achieving 92% retrieval precision.[^1]

  • Problem & Context
    Explain the difficulty of quickly extracting sector insights from large volumes of financial news and the need for fast, explainable answers rather than black‑box predictions.[^1]

  • Data & Indexing

    • 500+ financial articles as corpus.
    • Preprocessing steps (cleaning, chunking, metadata tagging).
    • Indexing into a vector database (ChromaDB) with semantic search.[^1]

  • Architecture & Stack

    • Sentence‑transformers for embeddings.
    • ChromaDB for vector search and retrieval.
    • LangChain orchestration with FinBERT sentiment analysis to enrich responses.
    • Hugging Face Spaces deployment containerized with Docker.[^1]

  • Evaluation & A/B Tests

    • Describe A/B tests on article count to find the optimal 100‑article window for quality vs. latency.
    • Summarize how retrieval precision reached 92% and latency stayed under 15 ms.[^1]

  • Results & Impact

    • Emphasize the time savings for research workflows.
    • Mention that the system is live and usable by others (if demo is public).

  • What Was Learned

    • Trade‑offs between context window size and latency.
    • Importance of evaluation frameworks for RAG rather than only “it feels good.”
    • Practical lessons on containerization and fast APIs.[^1]

MLOps Loan Default Prediction with Drift Monitoring – Deep‑Dive Page

This project shows MLOps maturity and end‑to‑end ML system design.[^1]

Suggested content:

  • TL;DR
    Built a production ML system that predicts loan defaults on 148K applications with 89% accuracy, 0.92 ROC‑AUC, and 0.86 F1‑score using Random Forest, deployed with a web interface on Hugging Face Spaces via Docker with <100 ms latency.[^1]

  • Problem & Context

    • Why predicting loan defaults matters (risk management, capital allocation).
    • Constraints such as speed and interpretability.

  • Data & Features

    • 148K applications, feature count, and types (demographics, financial variables, etc.).
    • Handling of imbalance, missing values, and leakage (if applicable).[^1]

  • Architecture & Stack

    • PyTorch data pipeline with Dataset and DataLoader achieving 4,600x memory reduction and 23x faster loading.[^1]
    • Random Forest model for primary predictions.
    • MLflow for experiment tracking and model versioning.
    • Drift monitoring using Population Stability Index across 51 features.
    • Deployment via Dockerized app on Hugging Face Spaces.[^1]

  • Monitoring & MLOps Practices

    • How PSI thresholds are set and interpreted.
    • How model retraining or alerts would be triggered.

  • Results & Impact

    • Stress metrics: accuracy, ROC‑AUC, F1‑score.
    • Latency (<100 ms) and scalability considerations.[^1]

  • What Was Learned

    • Importance of data pipelines for scalability.
    • Lessons from integrating experiment tracking and drift into a cohesive workflow.

GymLog – Full‑Stack Fitness Tracking Web App – Deep‑Dive Page

GymLog is ideal for demonstrating full‑stack engineering plus ML integration.[^2]

Suggested content:

  • TL;DR
    GymLog is a production‑ready, full‑stack fitness tracking web app built with Python Flask and PostgreSQL, featuring workout logging, nutrition tracking, body metrics, and a machine learning‑powered progressive overload prediction model.[^2]

  • Stack & Architecture

    • Backend: Flask 3.0, SQLAlchemy ORM, Flask‑Login, Flask‑Migrate, PostgreSQL/SQLite support.[^2]
    • Frontend: Bootstrap 5, vanilla JavaScript with Fetch API, Chart.js for visualizations, glassmorphism UI with dark mode.[^2]
    • Deployment: Gunicorn with cloud hosting (Render/Fly.io) and environment‑based configuration.[^2]

  • Core Features

    • Workout tracking with sets, reps, weight, rest timers, templates, and calendar visualization.
    • Advanced training features: supersets, drop sets, exercise normalization, rest timer.[^2]
    • Nutrition tracking with dual‑API integration (OpenFoodFacts and USDA FoodData Central), barcode scanning, relevance scoring, and multi‑unit support.[^2]
    • Body metrics and analytics, consistency tracking, dashboards with quick stats.[^2]

  • Notable Implementations

    • Exercise name normalization via regex patterns, abbreviation expansions, and reordering to manage 100+ exercise variations.[^2]
    • Dual‑API food search with a custom relevance scoring algorithm (phrase matching, word presence, length penalties) and intelligent fallback between OpenFoodFacts and USDA.[^2]
    • Gradient Boosting Regressor for progressive overload predictions with engineered temporal and volume features, confidence intervals, and smart rounding to 2.5 lb increments.[^2]

  • Results & Scale

    • ~11,500+ lines of code including app, models, templates, ML, and utilities.[^2]
    • 40+ REST endpoints, 12 data models, 6 migration versions, and multi‑user architecture.[^2]

  • What Was Learned

    • Lessons on designing normalized schemas, integrating multiple external APIs, and deploying a complex app to production.

Airbnb Market Prediction – San Francisco – Deep‑Dive Page

This project highlights classical supervised learning, EDA, and business insights using R.[^3]

Suggested content:

  • TL;DR
    Built supervised ML models to predict which San Francisco Airbnb listings will achieve high booking rates using a 12,947‑listing dataset, with a Random Forest model achieving 95% ROC‑AUC.[^3]

  • Problem & Context

    • Help hosts and platform teams understand drivers of high booking rates (pricing, property features, host behavior, reviews).[^3]

  • Data & Preprocessing

    • Source: Inside Airbnb dataset.
    • 12,947 total properties, 6,000‑listing sample in the repo.[^3]
    • 32 numerical and categorical features including property attributes, pricing, host metrics, and review scores.[^3]
    • Steps: price normalization, binary encoding, missing value imputation, feature selection.[^3]

  • Models & Evaluation

    • Models: Logistic Regression (baseline), Random Forest (best), LightGBM, Bagged Trees.[^3]
    • Primary metric ROC‑AUC; Random Forest at 95%, Bagged Trees 94%, LightGBM 93%, Logistic Regression 59%.[^3]

  • Business Insights

    • High‑booking properties offer ~14.3% weekly discounts vs. 5% industry average.[^3]
    • Each additional bedroom increases odds of high booking by roughly 65%.[^3]
    • Optimal pricing around 5–10% below market median with generous weekly discounts.[^3]

  • What Was Learned

    • Trade‑offs between interpretable models and ensemble performance.
    • How to translate model outputs into actionable pricing and discount strategies.[^3]
