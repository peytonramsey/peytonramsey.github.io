Energy Market Data Analyst AI Agent

Natural-language exploration of FERC natural gas transaction data

Role: Sole Developer · February 2026 – In Progress
Project Vision

Building an AI-powered analyst that ingests and analyzes FERC (Federal Energy Regulatory Commission) natural gas transaction data, enabling natural-language exploration of multi-year volume trends, counterparty activity, and market dynamics. The goal is to make a complex, structured government dataset queryable in plain English,turning raw pipeline transaction records into accessible market intelligence.

Why This Project

Domain Context

FERC Form 552 data contains annual natural gas transaction records from major pipeline companies,buyer, seller, counterparty, volume, price, and pipeline location. This data is public but notoriously difficult to query: it spans multiple years, heterogeneous formats, and requires domain knowledge to interpret meaningfully.

Questions like "Which counterparties have increased their purchases on the Texas Eastern Transmission pipeline over the past 3 years?" or "What's the volume trend for LDC buyers in the Northeast?" currently require manual data wrangling across multiple CSV files and domain familiarity with FERC filing conventions. An AI agent with tool use can dramatically lower the barrier to entry for this analysis.

This project also connects directly to experience from Sprague Operating Resources (commodity data analysis),the energy domain is familiar, making it possible to build something practically useful rather than academically interesting.

Why Pydantic AI?

Pydantic AI provides a Pythonic, type-safe framework for building production-quality AI agents. Unlike LangChain (complex orchestration) or raw function-calling (no validation), Pydantic AI enforces structured outputs through Pydantic models,meaning the agent's data extraction results are validated at the type level before being processed. For a data analyst agent where correct schema interpretation is critical, this prevents silent data errors.
