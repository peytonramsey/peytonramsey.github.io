Macroeconomic Anomaly Detection

A deployed LSTM Autoencoder that flags structural breaks in the macro environment, grounded in 32 years of FRED data

Role: Sole Developer · Spring 2026 · Production · Deployed
TL;DR

Built a deployed macroeconomic anomaly detection system that ingests 7 FRED indicators in real time, runs a multivariate LSTM Autoencoder, and flags periods where the macro environment deviates from learned normal patterns. The anomaly threshold is calibrated on a pre-2007 baseline (mean + 2 std = 0.0203), and the model correctly identified all three target events: the 2008 GFC, COVID-19 shock, and 2022 Fed rate cycle, with minimal false positives in the 1990-2007 baseline. Flagged windows are passed to Llama-3.1-8B for plain-English explanation. Live on Hugging Face Spaces via Gradio with FastAPI and MLflow tracking.

Problem & Context

The Fintech Risk Team Problem

Fintech risk teams, especially those running anomaly detection and macro risk monitoring, follow a core loop: flag changes, identify root causes, generate response rules. The challenge is that macroeconomic regime shifts don't announce themselves. They emerge from the co-movement of multiple indicators: credit spreads widen as unemployment rises while the Fed cuts rates simultaneously. No single series captures that.

Monitoring 7 macro indicators independently fails to capture those joint dynamics. The 2008 GFC, for example, wasn't a credit spread anomaly or an unemployment anomaly in isolation; it was the simultaneous, correlated deterioration across all of them that signaled a genuine regime shift. A univariate model on any single series would have missed it or flagged it late.

The goal was a system that learns the normal co-movement structure across macro indicators and flags deviations from that structure as a single anomaly signal, along with a plain-English explanation of what likely drove the deviation.

This project also builds directly on a prior VECM analysis in R on 4 of the same FRED series, which identified 3 cointegrating relationships among CPI, Industrial Production, Retail Sales, and Unemployment. That finding confirmed the series share long-run equilibrium dynamics and directly motivated the multivariate design: rather than 7 independent models, one joint model learns the co-movement structure.

My Approach

LSTM Autoencoder Over Forecasting

The standard LSTM setup for time series is a forecasting model: predict the next step and flag large prediction errors as anomalies. That approach was ruled out for two reasons. First, macro series are noisy enough that forecasting errors are high even in normal conditions, making the threshold unstable. Second, a forecasting model is directional: it flags a specific value being wrong, not a structural deviation in the joint pattern.

An autoencoder inverts this: train the model to reconstruct normal sequences, not predict future ones. At inference time, high reconstruction error means the current window doesn't look like anything the model learned during training, which is the anomaly signal. The MSE across all 7 features and all 12 time steps collapses into a single number per window that can be directly compared to a stable threshold.

The threshold calibration approach was also deliberate. An initial attempt using percentile-based thresholds (85th, 95th) on the full reconstruction error distribution produced nearly identical values because the crisis events in the training data distort the upper tail. The fix was calibrating on the pre-2007 baseline only, representing stable macro conditions before any major crisis entered the data. Mean + 2 std on that calm period gives a threshold that reflects genuine normal variation without being inflated by the events the model is trying to detect.

Data

7 FRED Indicators

All data is pulled via fredapi from the Federal Reserve Economic Database. The 7 indicators were selected to cover the major transmission channels of macroeconomic stress: price regime, monetary policy, labor market, demand-side activity, long-term rates, credit risk, and market fear.

Monthly frequency. Training window: January 1990 to December 2021 (32 years). Test window: January 2022 to present. A MinMaxScaler is fit on the training data only and applied to all other windows, preventing data leakage. Missing values are dropped after aligning all series on the date index. Sequences are created with a 12-month sliding window (shape: batch x 12 x 7), matching the monthly granularity and capturing annual macro cycles.

Key Design Decisions

  • ▸Autoencoder vs forecasting LSTM: reconstruction error is a stable, threshold-able anomaly signal; forecasting error is noisy and directional
  • ▸Multivariate (7 series): VECM analysis confirmed cointegration across 4 of these series, justifying a joint model that captures co-movement structure
  • ▸Pre-2007 threshold calibration: percentile-based thresholds on the full distribution were distorted by the crisis events in training data; the calm baseline isolates genuine normal variation
  • ▸MinMaxScaler fit on train only: prevents future price levels from bleeding into the scaler's normalization range (data leakage)
  • ▸12-month window: matches monthly data granularity and captures annual macro cycles; short enough to keep sequence length tractable
  • ▸Inline inference in app.py: app.py fetches FRED data and runs inference directly instead of calling a local FastAPI server, making it self-contained for HF Spaces deployment
Threshold Calibration

Why 0.0203

The anomaly threshold is the most important hyperparameter in this system because it determines the false positive rate. Two approaches were tried before landing on the final one.

Attempt 1: percentile-based. Thresholds at the 85th and 95th percentiles of reconstruction error across the full history produced values of 0.1565 and 0.1571, nearly identical. The reason: crisis periods like 2008 and 2020 inflate the upper tail of the distribution, compressing the difference between percentile cutoffs. Neither threshold correctly flagged all three known events.

Attempt 2: hardcoded. Setting threshold = 0.04 by visual inspection of the reconstruction error plot worked in the sense that it flagged the right events, but it was unprincipled and would not generalize.

Final: pre-2007 calm baseline. Restrict the reference distribution to the period before any major crises entered the training data (1990 to 2007). Compute mean + 2 std on that subset. The result is 0.0203, a threshold grounded in what normal reconstruction error looks like during genuinely stable macro conditions. The pre-2007 period shows minimal false positives, and all three target events exceed it with clear margin.

The 2022 Fed shock is the most interesting case. Unlike 2008 and COVID (discrete shocks with clear onset and recovery), the Fed rate hiking cycle produced a persistent regime shift in reconstruction error. The model doesn't just spike; it stays elevated through 2023-2025, reflecting that the macro environment hasn't returned to the learned normal. That pattern is real information: the system is correctly distinguishing between a temporary shock and a structural change in the macro regime.

Explanation Layer

LLM Post-Processing via Llama-3.1-8B

When the model flags anomalies in a selected date range, the flagged window dates and summary statistics are passed to meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct via the Hugging Face Inference API. The model returns a 2-3 sentence plain-English explanation of the likely macroeconomic cause.

This layer is intentionally thin. The prompt passes only the flagged dates, the threshold value, and the total anomaly count, so the LLM is doing pattern matching against its macro knowledge, not data analysis. The goal is interpretability for the user, not a second layer of modeling.

You are a macroeconomic analyst. An LSTM anomaly detection model
flagged the following months as anomalous: [dates].
The anomaly threshold was {threshold}.
There were {n_anomalies} anomalies total.
In 2-3 sentences, explain what macroeconomic events likely caused these anomalies.

The final model selection was Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct after two prior models failed: Mistral-7B did not support chat completion via the HF Inference API, and zephyr-7b-beta was not supported by any active provider. Llama-3.1-8B works on both local and HF Spaces environments via the same token.

What I Learned

Key Takeaways

  • 01.
    Threshold calibration matters more than architecture. The LSTM architecture was straightforward. Getting the threshold right required three attempts and understanding why percentile-based approaches fail on distributions that contain the events you're trying to detect.
  • 02.
    Prior statistical work transfers in non-obvious ways. The VECM cointegration finding didn't port as code, but it ported as architectural justification. Knowing the series are cointegrated is the reason to use one multivariate model instead of 7 univariate ones.
  • 03.
    Deployment constraints shape design. The FastAPI layer was built first, then dropped from the deployed version when it became clear that HF Spaces doesn't run a local uvicorn server. The final app.py runs inference inline, making it self-contained.
  • 04.
    Persistent regime shifts look different from discrete shocks. The 2022 Fed shock produces sustained elevated reconstruction error, not a spike-and-recovery. That's real information the model is surfacing, and it changes how you interpret the plot.
  • 05.
    Encoding issues cause silent failures late. The UTF-16-encoded requirements.txt passed local checks but broke the HF Spaces build with character-level pip errors. Small environment mismatches surface at the worst time.
