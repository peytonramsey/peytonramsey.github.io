Problem & Approach

The Fintech Risk Team Problem

Fintech risk teams follow a core loop: flag changes, identify root causes, generate response rules. The challenge is that macroeconomic regime shifts don't announce themselves. They emerge from the co-movement of multiple indicators simultaneously. The 2008 GFC wasn't a credit spread anomaly in isolation; it was the correlated deterioration across credit, unemployment, and rates at the same time. A univariate model on any single series would have missed it or flagged it late. A prior VECM analysis confirmed cointegration among 4 of these FRED series, motivating a joint multivariate model over 7 independent ones.

The standard LSTM for time series predicts the next step and flags large prediction errors. That approach was ruled out because macro series are noisy enough that forecasting errors are high even in normal conditions. An autoencoder inverts this: train the model to reconstruct normal sequences. High reconstruction error at inference means the current window doesn't match anything from training. That's the anomaly signal, stable and threshold-able.

Data

7 FRED Indicators

All data is pulled via fredapi from the Federal Reserve Economic Database. The 7 indicators were selected to cover the major transmission channels of macroeconomic stress: price regime, monetary policy, labor market, demand-side activity, long-term rates, credit risk, and market fear.

Threshold Calibration

Why 0.0203

The anomaly threshold is the most important hyperparameter in this system because it determines the false positive rate. Two approaches were tried before landing on the final one.

Attempt 1: percentile-based. Thresholds at the 85th and 95th percentiles of reconstruction error across the full history produced values of 0.1565 and 0.1571, nearly identical. The reason is that crisis periods like 2008 and 2020 inflate the upper tail of the distribution, compressing the difference between percentile cutoffs. Neither threshold correctly flagged all three known events.

Attempt 2: hardcoded. Setting threshold = 0.04 by visual inspection of the reconstruction error plot worked in the sense that it flagged the right events, but it was unprincipled and would not generalize.

Final: pre-2007 calm baseline. Restrict the reference distribution to the period before any major crises entered the training data (1990 to 2007). Compute mean + 2 std on that subset. The result is 0.0203, a threshold grounded in what normal reconstruction error looks like during genuinely stable macro conditions. The pre-2007 period shows minimal false positives, and all three target events exceed it with clear margin.

Ground-Truth Validation

Three Structurally Different Crises

Event Period What drove it Result
2008 GFC Sep 2008 – Mar 2009 Credit spread spike, Fed to zero, unemployment surge Flagged
COVID-19 Mar 2020 – Jun 2020 Unemployment to 14.7%, VIX to 80, retail sales collapse Flagged
2022 Fed Shock Mar 2022 – Dec 2022 Rapid rate hiking cycle, sustained regime shift Flagged

The 2022 Fed shock is the most interesting case. Unlike 2008 and COVID (discrete shocks with clear onset and recovery), the Fed rate hiking cycle produced a persistent regime shift in reconstruction error. The model doesn't just spike; it stays elevated through 2023–2025, reflecting that the macro environment hasn't returned to the learned normal. The system is correctly distinguishing between a temporary shock and a structural change in the macro regime.

Explanation Layer

LLM Post-Processing via Llama-3.1-8B

When the model flags anomalies in a selected date range, the flagged window dates and summary statistics are passed to meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct via the Hugging Face Inference API. The model returns a 2-3 sentence plain-English explanation of the likely macroeconomic cause.

This layer is intentionally thin because the goal is easy interpretation. The prompt passes only the flagged dates, the threshold value, and the total anomaly count, so the LLM is doing pattern matching against its macro knowledge.

You are a macroeconomic analyst. An LSTM anomaly detection model
flagged the following months as anomalous: [dates].
The anomaly threshold was {threshold}.
There were {n_anomalies} anomalies total.
In 2-3 sentences, explain what macroeconomic events likely caused these anomalies.
WHAT I LEARNED

Training on pre-crisis data and testing on held-out crises is intellectually satisfying, but it's not a real backtest. The model "identified" 2008, 2020, and the 2022 rate cycle because I designed it to be sensitive to exactly the kind of multivariate divergence those events caused. The honest question is whether it would have flagged them in real time, before the narrative was clear, and I genuinely don't know. That uncertainty is now how I evaluate all anomaly detection work.
