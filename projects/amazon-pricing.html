Strategy Analysis

Exploratory analysis of 1.4 million Amazon products to surface pricing dynamics across 248 categories — uncovering the Discount Paradox: best sellers carry systematically lower discounts than the products surrounding them.

Peyton Ramsey · 1.4M products · 248 categories · Sole Analyst · 2024
TL;DR

Ran exploratory data analysis across 1.4 million Amazon product listings spanning 248 categories. The central finding — the Discount Paradox — is that best sellers average materially lower discount rates than non-best-seller products in the same categories. Sentiment segmentation by review text further reinforced this: high-volume products compete on quality signal and positioning, not on perpetual price cuts. The analysis surfaces a systematic flaw in the assumption that aggressive discounting drives Amazon sales rank.

Problem & Approach

The conventional wisdom in e-commerce is that discounting drives sales rank. Sellers routinely slash prices to accumulate the reviews and velocity needed to reach best seller status, then raise prices once the badge is secured. This analysis tests whether that pattern actually shows up in the data at scale — or whether best sellers look structurally different from their discounted peers.

The dataset is a large-scale Amazon product scrape containing listing price, original price, discount percentage, category, star rating, review count, and best seller status. At 1.4M rows, the sample is large enough that patterns which emerge are not artifacts of small-N noise — they represent real distributional differences in how the marketplace operates.

The approach is deliberately exploratory rather than model-driven. When the question is "what does the data actually show?", adding a predictive model introduces complexity without adding interpretability. The findings here are direct distributional comparisons, which makes them defensible to a non-technical audience and resistant to the usual objections about model assumptions.

Sentiment segmentation was added to test whether review text corroborates the pricing structure: if best sellers are winning on quality signal rather than discount depth, their review language should reflect different dimensions of value than the heavily-discounted remainder of the catalog.

Data & Features

The dataset is a large-scale Amazon product scrape. Each row is a single listing at a point in time, with no temporal dimension — this is a cross-sectional snapshot. The best seller flag is binary: a listing either holds a best seller badge in its category or it does not.

Pricing Fields

Listed price, original price, computed discount percentage, currency normalization across markets

Sales Rank Signal

Best seller flag (binary), category assignment, rank within category where available

Quality Signals

Star rating (1–5), review count, verified purchase ratio where present

Review Text

Aggregated review content used for sentiment and keyword frequency segmentation by best seller status

Preprocessing Notes
Price string parsing was required — raw fields contained currency symbols and formatting inconsistencies. Discount percentage was computed directly from listed vs. original price rather than trusting the scraped discount field, which had missing values. Listings with zero or negative prices were excluded. Category labels were standardized to collapse near-duplicate category names before segmentation.
Key Findings
01.
The Discount Paradox holds across categories. Best sellers carry lower average discount rates than non-best-seller products in the same categories. The pattern is consistent enough across 248 categories to be structural — not driven by a handful of electronics or household staples. Products that achieve top sales rank are not doing so by undercutting on price.
02.
Review count outweighs discount depth as a quality signal proxy. When segmenting by best seller status, review volume diverges sharply between the two groups. Best sellers have accumulated significantly more reviews, suggesting that social proof and marketplace velocity — not perpetual discounting — are the primary drivers of sustained rank.
03.
Sentiment language differs between best sellers and the discounted remainder. Review text for best sellers skews toward product quality, durability, and fit-for-purpose descriptors. The heavily-discounted non-best-sellers attract more reviews that surface pricing and value-for-money language — consistent with buyers who made purchase decisions based primarily on price signals.
04.
Star rating distributions are compressed and do not separate the groups cleanly. Both best sellers and non-best-sellers cluster above 4.0. Rating alone is not a discriminating feature — this corroborates the pricing finding that something other than quality score determines rank. The signal is in volume and velocity, not rating level.

If I Were To Revisit It
01.
Add a temporal dimension to test whether the Discount Paradox is causal or coincidental. The current dataset is a point-in-time snapshot. The Paradox could reflect products that achieved rank first and then raised prices — or products that never discounted and still reached the top. Tracking listings over time would distinguish between these two mechanisms, which have very different strategic implications for sellers.
02.
Apply a proper NLP pipeline to the review text rather than keyword frequency counts. The sentiment analysis here is frequency-based — a more rigorous approach would use topic modeling (LDA or BERTopic) to surface latent review themes rather than surface-level word occurrence. The directional finding holds, but the nuance in what buyers are actually saying would be richer with embedding-based methods.
03.
Control for category price floor when comparing discount rates. A 15% discount on a $500 item and a 15% discount on a $12 item are not equivalent strategic decisions. The current analysis compares percentage discounts without controlling for absolute price level or category price distribution. Normalizing within price tiers within categories would sharpen the Paradox finding and separate discount strategy from price positioning.
