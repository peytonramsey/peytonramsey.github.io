Problem & Approach

The core modeling problem is binary classification: given a listing's features, will it achieve a high booking rate (conversion rate > 5%)? Part I framed this at national scale using a Kaggle competition dataset of 374K US listings. Part II zoomed into Paris's Popincourt quarter to test whether a gentrifying market inverts the rules found nationally, specifically whether commercial investment strategies that succeed in markets like Miami fail in a neighborhood shaped by rent control, tourist regulation, and cultural homogeneity.

Three research questions drove Part II: Is price the primary lever for booking success? Does host type independently predict performance beyond what price explains? Does within-neighborhood geography create exploitable micro-market advantages? Each question was answered through EDA first, then validated by SHAP importance rankings from the trained model.

CatBoost was chosen for its native categorical encoding, which avoids ordinal encoding leakage, and its ordered target statistics, which prevent within-fold target leakage during training. Optuna (40 Bayesian trials) tuned hyperparameters. The central engineering challenge throughout was leakage prevention: TF-IDF and SVD were fit separately inside each CV fold, and neighborhood booking rates were computed out-of-fold with Bayesian shrinkage (λ=30) to avoid the target bleeding into features.

Data & Preprocessing

Two datasets from Inside Airbnb at very different scales: a national US snapshot for Part I and a longitudinal Popincourt panel for Part II. Feature engineering was the majority of the effort, 75+ features across 9 categories, with each category built for a specific predictive rationale rather than added opportunistically.

Part I: US National

374K listings, Kaggle train+test format, 56 raw columns. Price capped at 99th percentile (~$565). Custom null handling in Polars. Class imbalance addressed with auto_class_weights=Balanced.

Part II: Paris Popincourt

93,578 listing-months, Inside Airbnb, 2015-2024 panel. 11th Arrondissement. Snapshot date and host cohort year extracted for temporal panel analysis. Host type segmented as casual (1 listing), semi-pro (2-5), commercial (6+).

Text NLP (Part I)

6 text columns combined per listing. TF-IDF: 5K features, bigrams, min_df=5, sublinear_tf. TruncatedSVD: 40 components. Both fit per fold inside CV to prevent leakage.

Leakage Prevention

Neighborhood booking rate computed out-of-fold with Bayesian shrinkage (λ=30). TF-IDF/SVD fit separately inside each CV fold. OOF fold structure matched exactly across all leaky features.

Domain-Informed Imputation
Superhost/communication-tier-based response rate imputation rather than mean fill. Instant_bookable-based acceptance rate imputation. Bedrooms = 0 for studios rather than treating as missing. Each choice preserves a signal that standard imputation discards.

Business Insights

The Popincourt analysis was structured around three investor questions. The answers are specific enough to be actionable, not just directionally correct.

01.
Price is the primary lever, not a secondary one. Budget listings book at 54.3%, premium at 22.1%. That's a 32-percentage-point spread that is monotonic across all tiers. Logistic regression coefficient on log_price = -1.126 (p < 0.001). Charging more in Popincourt dramatically reduces booking rate; this is not the result you'd expect in most markets.
02.
Commercial operators structurally underperform at every price tier. Commercial hosts (6+ listings) book at 11.5% vs. 44.2% for casual hosts, a 6× gap. Cross-tab confirms it isn't price-driven: commercial + budget = 13.0% vs. casual + budget = 59.1%. Host type ranks #11 in SHAP (mean |SHAP| 0.110) after controlling for four price features. 15 years of market data show no convergence. This is structural, not cyclical.
03.
The Paris 120-night cap workaround backfires for commercial operators. Commercial hosts using min_nights ≥ 30 to reclassify as medium-term rentals book at 6.3%, the worst cell in the entire dataset. Casual hosts with the same flag book at 69.7% ($81/night). The regulatory escape hatch doesn't rescue the operating profile; it compounds the underperformance.
04.
Geography is not a lever in Popincourt. K-means clustering (k=3 and k=4) correlates with booking rate at 0.018, effectively zero. Only 4 percentage points of booking rate variation across all spatial clusters. Haversine distances to the three gentrification anchors (Oberkampf, Bastille, Roquette) rank #19-27 in SHAP. No sub-neighborhood delivers structurally higher conversion.
If I Were To Revisit It
01.
Engineer temporal features more carefully. Snapshot year ranked #2 in SHAP (mean |SHAP| 0.700), higher than any investor-actionable feature. That's a COVID-era artifact from low-activity listings concentrated in 2020-2021, not a generalizable signal. A revisit would either exclude pandemic years or build explicit COVID-flag interactions rather than letting the model absorb temporal variance as a black-box year effect.
02.
Test time-series cross-validation instead of random splits. The 80/20 random split on a 2015-2024 panel means the model can train on future listing-months to predict past ones. Temporal CV (train on years 1-N, validate on year N+1) would give a more realistic estimate of whether the 0.966 AUC generalizes forward rather than just fitting panel-level patterns.
03.
Extend the host type analysis to pricing dynamics over time. The EDA established that commercial hosts entered the market at the same time as casual hosts (2009-2011) and have held flat market share since 2012. What's missing is whether commercial pricing has been converging toward casual pricing over time, or whether the gap is stable. A price disparity regression by host_type × year would answer whether the structural underperformance is narrowing.
WHAT I LEARNED

The two-scale structure of this project was intentional: start with a national model to establish patterns, then drill into one neighborhood to stress-test them. The 6× booking rate gap between casual and commercial hosts that price and geography can't explain is the kind of finding that only shows up when you get specific enough. Kaggle-scale data gives you coverage; neighborhood-scale data gives you signal. I'll use this approach deliberately going forward.
