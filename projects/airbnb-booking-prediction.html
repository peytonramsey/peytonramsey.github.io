Airbnb: US Market Classifier & Paris Investment Study

Built a national CatBoost classifier on 374K US listings to establish baseline Airbnb booking patterns, then retrained it hyperlocal on Popincourt, Paris to answer a specific investor question: does gentrification change who wins in this market? The answer had nothing to do with price or location.

Problem & Approach

The core modeling problem is binary classification: given a listing's features, will it achieve a high booking rate (conversion rate > 5%)? Part I framed this at national scale using 374K US listings. Part II zoomed into Paris's Popincourt quarter to test whether a gentrifying market inverts the rules found nationally in the US market, specifically whether commercial investment strategies that succeed in markets like Miami fail in a neighborhood shaped by rent control, tourist regulation, and cultural homogeneity.

Three research questions drove Part II, each testable through EDA and validated by SHAP importance rankings.

1. Is price the primary lever controlling booking success?

2. Does host type independently explain booking performance, or is it just a pricing proxy?

3. Does geography within Popincourt create exploitable micro-market advantages?

CatBoost was chosen for its native categorical encoding, which avoids ordinal encoding leakage, and its ordered target statistics, which prevent within-fold target leakage during training. Optuna (40 Bayesian trials) tuned hyperparameters. The central engineering challenge throughout was leakage prevention, TF-IDF and SVD were fit separately inside each CV fold, and neighborhood booking rates were computed out-of-fold with Bayesian shrinkage (λ=30) to avoid the target bleeding into features.

Data & Preprocessing

Two datasets from Inside Airbnb at very different scales, one a national US snapshot for Part I and the other a longitudinal Popincourt panel for Part II. Understanding the data, what each feature meant, and how to best engineer them was the majority of the effort. About 75+ features across 9 categories were created, carefully crafted to capture different aspects of the Airbnb ecosystem.

  • Part I (US National). 374K listings, Kaggle train+test format, 56 raw columns. Price capped at 99th percentile (~$565). Custom null handling in Polars. Class imbalance addressed with auto_class_weights=Balanced.
  • Part II (Paris Popincourt). 93,578 listing-months, 2015-2024 panel. Snapshot date and host cohort year extracted for temporal panel analysis. Host type segmented as casual (1 listing), semi-pro (2–5), commercial (6+).
  • Text NLP (Part I). 6 text columns combined per listing. TF-IDF with 5K features, bigrams, min_df=5, sublinear_tf. TruncatedSVD to 40 components. Both fit per fold inside CV to prevent leakage.
  • Leakage prevention. Neighborhood booking rate computed out-of-fold with Bayesian shrinkage (λ=30). TF-IDF/SVD fit separately inside each CV fold. OOF fold structure matched exactly across all leaky features.
Domain-Informed Imputation
Superhost/communication-tier-based response rate imputation rather than mean fill. Instant_bookable-based acceptance rate imputation. Bedrooms = 0 for studios rather than treating as missing. Each choice preserves a signal that standard imputation discards.
Models & Evaluation
  • Popincourt model (Part II). 0.966 holdout AUC. Threshold swept across 0.20-0.78 and set at 0.54 for best F1, yielding accuracy 0.91, precision 0.88, recall 0.88, F1 0.88. 34 features, depth=9.
  • US National model (Part I). 0.8668 on the Kaggle leaderboard, 0.8516 on a leakage-safe 5-fold stratified CV OOF estimate. 78 features, depth=10, tuned with Optuna over 40 Bayesian trials.
Business Insights

The Popincourt analysis was structured around three investor questions:

01.
Price is the primary lever, not a secondary one. Budget listings book at 54.3%, premium at 22.1%. That's a 32-percentage-point spread that is uniform across all tiers. Charging more in Popincourt dramatically reduces booking rate; this is not the result you'd expect in most markets.
02.
Commercial operators structurally underperform at every price tier. Commercial hosts (6+ listings) book at 11.5% vs. 44.2% for casual hosts, a 6× gap. Cross-tab confirms it isn't price-driven, but which commercial + budget = 13.0% vs. casual + budget = 59.1%. Host type ranks #11 in SHAP after controlling for four price features. 15 years of market data show no convergence. This is structural, not cyclical.
03.
The Paris 120-night cap workaround backfires for commercial operators. Commercial hosts using min_nights ≥ 30 to reclassify as medium-term rentals (to combat strict Paris restrictions) book at merely 6.3%. Casual hosts with the same flag book at 69.7%. This regulatory "hack" is actually counterproductive.
04.
Geography is not a lever in Popincourt. K-means clustering (k=3 and k=4) correlates with booking rate at 0.018, effectively zero. Only 4 percentage points of booking rate variation across all spatial clusters. Haversine distances to the three gentrification anchors (Oberkampf, Bastille, Roquette) rank #19-27 in SHAP.
Investor Recommendations

What the data says to do (and what to avoid)

01.
Budget-to-mid pricing wins. Listings at $48-$79 convert at the highest rates. Premium pricing signals the wrong thing in this market and costs bookings.
02.
Operate like a resident. Casual hosts outperform commercial operators at every price tier. Single listing, personal profile, lower price. The gap has not narrowed in 15 years of market data.
03.
Don't pay a location premium within the neighborhood. Very little correlation with booking and location. Sub-neighborhood geography is not an investment variable in Popincourt. Spend that capital on pricing strategy instead.
04.
Avoid commercial and premium together. Commercial hosts at premium price points book at 9.5%, the worst combination in the dataset. Using minimum nights ≥ 30 as a regulatory workaround makes it worse, dropping commercial operators to 6.3%.
If I Were To Revisit It
01.
Engineer temporal features more carefully. Snapshot year ranked #2 in SHAP, higher than any investor-actionable feature. That's a COVID-era artifact from low-activity listings concentrated in 2020-2021, not a generalizable signal. A revisit would either exclude pandemic years or build explicit COVID-flag interactions rather than letting the model absorb temporal variance as a black-box year effect.
02.
Test time-series cross-validation instead of random splits. The 80/20 random split on a 2015-2024 panel means the model can train on future listing-months to predict past ones. Temporal CV (train on years 1-N, validate on year N+1) would give a more realistic estimate of whether the 0.966 AUC generalizes forward rather than just fitting panel-level patterns.
03.
Extend the host type analysis to pricing dynamics over time. The EDA established that commercial hosts entered the market at the same time as casual hosts (2009-2011) and have held flat market share since 2012. What's missing is whether commercial pricing has been converging toward casual pricing over time, or whether the gap is stable. A price disparity regression by host_type × year would answer whether the structural underperformance is narrowing.
WHAT I LEARNED

The biggest thing this project reinforced is that you have to actually research the domain before you touch the data. Understanding how Inside Airbnb structures its datasets let me extract snapshot dates from picture URL columns, temporal information that wasn't available as a clean feature anywhere else in the data. That kind of thing only happens when you read the documentation, understand the source, and look for what's implicit rather than just what's labeled. The other part is being okay when the data contradicts your starting assumptions. Going in, price and location were the obvious levers. They weren't. Adapting the analysis around what the data was actually showing, rather than defending the original hypotheses, is where the real finding came from.
