U.S. Inflation Dynamics:
A Vector Error Correction Model Analysis

Modeling how demand, supply, and labor shocks dynamically transmit to U.S. inflation, revealing long-run equilibrium relationships that univariate models and VAR on differences cannot detect.

407 monthly observations · FRED Database · Jan 1992 – Oct 2024 · Spring 2026 · DS 809
TL;DR

A Vector Error Correction Model on 33 years of monthly FRED data reveals that demand (retail sales, 35.5%) and inflation inertia (own-shock, 38.4%) dominate long-run CPI variance, while labor market effects work through cointegrating equilibrium channels rather than short-run dynamics. A VAR on differences, the naive alternative, attributed 95.68% of CPI variance to its own shocks, missing 61.6% of the external transmission the VECM captures.

Problem & Context

Why Standard Models Miss Inflation Dynamics

Inflation is not just an autoregressive process. It's the output of interacting macroeconomic forces: consumer demand driving prices up, industrial production expanding supply, labor market slack moderating wage growth, all operating across different time horizons simultaneously.

A univariate model (ARIMA on CPI alone) captures inflation's persistence but attributes everything to its own past values. A VAR on differenced data respects stationarity but destroys the long-run relationships between variables in the process. The right tool when series are non-stationary but move together over time is a Vector Error Correction Model, which models both short-run dynamics and the long-run equilibria that tie the variables together.

The core research question: How do demand, supply, and labor market shocks dynamically affect U.S. inflation, and through what channels?

Dataset

Four FRED Series, 33 Years of Monthly Data

All series sourced from the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis FRED database, seasonally adjusted, January 1992 – October 2024.

CPI (CPIAUCSL)

Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers, the primary outcome variable measuring inflation.

Industrial Production (INDPRO)

Supply-side proxy. Represents the economy's productive capacity; expansions should dampen price pressure.

Advance Retail Sales (RSXFS)

Demand-side proxy. Consumer spending drives demand-pull inflation, the dominant transmission channel found in this analysis.

Unemployment Rate (UNRATE)

Labor market slack indicator. Higher unemployment should moderate wage growth and inflation, but operates through long-run equilibrium, not short-run dynamics.

Methodology

The Two-Step Path to VECM

Model selection followed a principled two-step process, each test ruling out a simpler approach before committing to VECM.

1

ADF Tests: Stationarity

Augmented Dickey-Fuller tests on all four series. Result: all four are I(1), non-stationary in levels and stationary after first differencing. This rules out VAR in levels (would be spurious) and suggests cointegration is possible.

2

Johansen Trace Test: Cointegration

Tests whether linear combinations of the non-stationary series are themselves stationary, i.e., whether the variables share a long-run equilibrium. Result: r = 3 cointegrating relationships among the four variables. This rules out VAR on differences (which discards the long-run information), justifying VECM.

3

VECM Estimation

The VECM decomposes each variable's movement into: (a) error correction terms, measuring how fast it corrects back toward long-run equilibrium when displaced, and (b) short-run dynamics, capturing how transitory shocks in other variables propagate. Lag order selected by BIC.

Intuition: What an Error Correction Term Does

If CPI drifts too far above its long-run equilibrium with retail sales and unemployment, the ECT coefficient (-0.0305) says CPI will fall back by 3% of that gap each month. This is the mechanism that prevents the variables from wandering apart indefinitely, and it's invisible in a VAR on differences.

Impulse Response Analysis

How Shocks Propagate to Inflation

Cholesky orthogonalized IRFs, 20-month horizon, 100 bootstrap runs for confidence intervals.

Retail Sales Shock → CPI

Near-linear increase in CPI over the full 20-month horizon: demand shocks don't dissipate quickly, they compound. This is the clearest evidence of demand-pull inflation dynamics in the data. IRFs remain non-zero at 20 months (vs. VAR convergence by 8-10 months).

Unemployment Shock → CPI

Insignificant in the short-run CPI equation but explains 20.5% of long-run variance. The signal lives in the error correction terms; labor market slack moderates inflation gradually through wage-price dynamics and equilibrium adjustment, not through immediate transmission.

Industrial Production Shock → CPI

Mild dampening effect: supply expansions apply modest downward pressure on prices, consistent with cost-side inflation theory. Only 5.6% of long-run CPI variance suggests supply constraints are less binding than demand conditions over this period.

Diagnostic Challenges

The COVID-19 Structural Break Problem

All three standard residual diagnostics reject their null hypotheses: Portmanteau (serial correlation), ARCH-LM (heteroscedasticity), and Jarque-Bera (normality). The root cause is the 2020–2022 COVID-19 period, an extreme structural shock that sits outside the equilibrium relationships estimated on 1992–2019 data.

▸Why the model is still valid: These violations affect inference precision but not the cointegrating structure itself. The 3 cointegrating vectors and ECT coefficients reflect genuine long-run relationships that hold outside the COVID shock window.
▸Mitigation: 100 bootstrap runs for IRF confidence intervals provide robust inference that doesn't rely on normality or homoscedasticity assumptions.
▸Proposed extension: Structural break dummy for 2020–2022, or a regime-switching VECM that allows the ECT coefficients to vary across calm vs. shock periods.
Key Findings

What the VECM Reveals

  • 01.
    Demand-pull inflation dominates long-run dynamics. Retail sales explains 35.5% of CPI variance at a 20-month horizon and produces a near-linear IRF. Consumer spending is the primary external driver of sustained inflation, more so than labor market conditions or supply capacity.
  • 02.
    Labor market effects are real but operate through equilibrium, not short-run dynamics. Unemployment is insignificant in the CPI short-run equation but accounts for 20.5% of long-run variance through error correction terms. The Phillips Curve is not dead; it works through cointegrating relationships over longer horizons.
  • 03.
    Supply-side effects are secondary over this period. Industrial production explains only 5.6% of long-run CPI variance. Supply constraints matter during specific episodes (COVID supply chain disruption) but are not the dominant inflation driver across the full 1992–2024 sample.
  • 04.
    Model selection matters enormously. The VAR on differences attributed 95.68% of CPI variance to own-shocks — appearing to confirm inflation is purely self-perpetuating. VECM reveals this is an artifact of discarding long-run information; the cointegrating structure is the mechanism through which macroeconomic forces actually operate.
