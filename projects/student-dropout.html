Problem & Approach

The Cost of Waiting Until It's Too Late

Student dropout is expensive on both sides. Universities lose tuition revenue and see funding tied to retention rates erode; students lose time, money, and momentum. The window for intervention — academic coaching, emergency financial aid, course load adjustment — only exists if you can identify risk early enough to act.

This project treats dropout prediction as a classification problem where the cost of a false negative (missing a real dropout) significantly outweighs the cost of a false positive (flagging a student who would have been fine). That asymmetry shaped every modeling decision — most importantly the choice to evaluate all models at both the default 0.5 threshold and a more aggressive 0.3 threshold.

The deployment scenario: run the model at the end of each student's first semester, flag students above the risk threshold, and route them to targeted support before the second semester begins.

Recall First, Then Model Selection

Four algorithms were evaluated at two classification thresholds because the threshold is a business decision that shouldn't be separated from model selection. Moving from 0.5 to 0.3 changes which students get flagged, and that choice belongs to the institution, not the modeler. The primary constraint was interpretability: the best-performing model also needs to explain which features drove the flag so advisors can have an informed conversation, which is why SVM was eliminated despite its lower test error. Recall on the dropout class was chosen as the primary metric before any model was run, not selected post-hoc to favor a preferred result.

4,424 students from a Portuguese higher education institution across multiple academic years. The original three-class outcome (Dropout / Enrolled / Graduate) was collapsed into a binary target: Dropout vs. Not Dropout.

Key EDA Findings
Bimodal grade distribution for dropouts. Dropout students show a sharp spike at zero grades in the first semester — many stopped showing up before completing any coursework.
Tuition fees are a strong binary signal. Students behind on tuition had an 85% dropout rate vs. 25% for students current on payments.
Scholarships significantly reduce dropout. Scholarship holders dropped out at 12.5% vs. 40% without scholarships.
Evening students at higher risk. 38.7% dropout rate vs. 31.2% for daytime students.

Model Selection

Four Models, Two Thresholds

All models were evaluated at both threshold=0.5 (default) and threshold=0.3 (aggressive, optimized for recall). The primary evaluation criteria were dropout-class recall and test error rate.

Logistic Regression
5 hand-selected predictors: tuition fees status, 1st semester grades, scholarship status, debtor status, admission grade. All significant at p < 0.01. Interpretable but linear decision boundary caps recall.
K-Nearest Neighbors (k=15)
Tuned k via 10-fold repeated CV. Can only use numeric predictors, excluding tuition fees and scholarship status — two of the strongest signals. Center + scale preprocessing applied to normalize distances.
Random Forest — Selected Model
mtry=4, ntree=500, both tuned via OOB error. Uses all predictors including categorical variables. Captures non-linear interactions between financial and academic signals. Best recall at both thresholds.
Support Vector Machine (Radial Kernel)
cost=10, gamma=0.01, tuned via 10-fold CV. Competitive error rate but no feature importance output — eliminated because advisors need to understand why a student was flagged.

Key Findings

What the Models Revealed

Non-linear interactions matter. The reason Random Forest beats Logistic Regression isn't just more predictors — the interaction between academic and financial distress is non-linear. A student behind on tuition AND with zero 1st semester approvals is dramatically higher risk than either signal alone suggests.
KNN's categorical exclusion cost it the race. KNN can't handle categorical predictors without encoding hacks, which forced exclusion of tuition status and scholarship holder — the 3rd and 4th most important predictors. This is a fundamental algorithm limitation, not a hyperparameter problem.
Threshold tuning is a business decision, not just a modeling one. Moving from 0.5 to 0.3 increased Random Forest recall from 79.23% to 89.79% — an extra 10 out of 100 true dropouts correctly flagged, at the cost of more false alarms. Whether that trade-off is worth it depends on advisor bandwidth and intervention cost, not test accuracy.
Evening students are better identified. Segment analysis showed the model achieved 92.7% recall on evening students vs. 89.3% on daytime — likely because evening students have stronger, less ambiguous predictor signals (more pronounced financial stress patterns).
If I Were to Revisit It

What I'd Do Differently

01.
Frame the evaluation metric before picking a model. Starting from "maximize recall, subject to error rate below X%" makes model selection much cleaner than comparing a table of numbers post-hoc. The business objective should define the metric, not the other way around.
02.
Interpretability is a real constraint. SVM had the lowest test error but was eliminated because advisors need to understand why a student was flagged. A model that can't explain itself won't get used, no matter how accurate.
03.
Mid-semester updates would sharpen predictions. The model currently runs on end-of-first-semester data, but payment status and attendance could be observed in real time. A version that updates risk scores continuously throughout the semester would catch struggling students earlier.
04.
Class imbalance deserves explicit treatment. We handled imbalance through stratified splitting and threshold tuning, but techniques like SMOTE or class-weighted loss could be worth testing — especially if the minority class shrinks in a smaller institution's dataset.
